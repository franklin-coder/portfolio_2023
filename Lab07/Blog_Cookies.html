Should Digital Cookies Be Regulated?

Could the internet spy on users’ privacy? Currently, the internet has turned into a vital tool in human beings’ daily routines, even for those who don't use it. Although it can bring a lot of benefits helping to solve almost any process from school or work task up to make friends or find the next wife, the last decade the use the internet has risen to 70 % between 1990 and 2016 worldwide, showing for example that 98 % of Iceland’s population has used the internet, while African countries with 20 % of the average consumption and the global total of users in 2016 depict 3.408 billion(Roser et al., 2015). However, there are some doubts to figure out, for example, why the internet has personal information from internet surfers like passwords or buys preferences, and how can it take that kind of information? Or where is put the data?Despite that cyberspace is known as an essential service nowadays, unfortunately, it can also be found some risks due to this technology bringing additional elements to work more efficiently, although the first email was sent in the 1960s, later in the 1970s the first file was able to be sent it, after that in 1982 internet began the, however in 1989 Tim Berners-Lee a British scientist changed the way people communicated forever creating the relevant impact and expansion endless yet (Roser et al., 2015). Despite this positive aspect, another group of individuals believes that features such as digital cookies can compromise online privacy and security.The term "cookie" refers to a small file containing a unique identifier that sends users' information from web servers to browsers as a piece of text, which is sometimes saved on the hard disk. This process can help to improve the website's usability, but it can also make browsing the internet feel unreliable. Although many websites today use "cookies" to remember passwords and usernames and track which sites a user visits (Hewlett-Packard Company, 2018).Many internet users, on the other hand, are concerned about the use of cookies to track their browsing habits. These concerned individuals are beginning to see cookies as an invasion of privacy (Xu & Zhang, 2016). As a result, the topic of this essay will be whether or not cookies should be regulated to protect users' privacy and personal data on the internet. Although three types of cookies were developed to improve the user experience: persistent, first-party, and third-party cookies, each of which can provide a certain level of security, there is also a cookie known as a super cookie that can obtain user data such as passwords and last websites visited while browsing the internet and send it to a different server (Xu & Zhang, 2016),although any case has been confirmed about identity fraud by digital cookies, it could be a huge opportunity to do it, and when someone tries to keep records or use tracking devices on others, privacy concerns arise(Miller, 2001).
